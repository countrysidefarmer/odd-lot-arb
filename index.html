How it works

How you make money

Odd-lot arbitrage is a low-risk, market-neutral strategy that exploits a quirk in how US tender offers handle small shareholders.

📋

What is a tender offer?

When a company wants to buy back its own shares, it can make a public offer — a "tender offer" — to purchase shares at a fixed price or within a price range, typically above the current market price.

⚡

The odd-lot privilege

US tender offer rules grant holders of fewer than 100 shares ("odd lots") priority acceptance — your shares are purchased in full before any proration applies to large shareholders. No lottery, no partial fills.

📐

The mechanics

Buy exactly 99 shares on the open market at the current price. Submit them in the tender offer at the offer price. Collect the spread. Settlement typically takes 3–5 business days after the offer closes.

🧮

The math

Max Profit = 99 × (offer price − market price). The table above shows this for each live opportunity. Subtract brokerage commission (usually <$5) to get your net.

Key risks to understand: (1) The company can withdraw the offer before it closes — rare but possible. (2) The stock price may rise above the offer price, making the trade unprofitable before you tender. (3) Dutch-auction offers have a range; you receive the clearing price, which may be below the upper bound shown. (4) Some brokers require manual submission — confirm your broker supports odd-lot tendering before trading.

Who qualifies for odd-lot priority?

You must hold fewer than 100 shares at the record date. This means you cannot have existing positions in the stock — or if you do, your total holding must still be under 100 shares after your purchase.

Most retail brokers (Fidelity, Schwab, IBKR, TD Ameritrade) support odd-lot tender. Call your broker to confirm they will submit your shares with the odd-lot election checked.

Dutch auction vs. fixed price

A fixed-price offer has one price — you know exactly what you'll receive. A Dutch auction shows a range (e.g. $36–$40); the company sets the final "clearing price" within that range after the offer closes.

Odd-lot holders in Dutch auctions are typically paid at the upper end of the final clearing price — check the specific offer document for the exact terms before trading.

Tax note: Profits from tender offer arbitrage are typically treated as short-term capital gains (held <1 year). Consult a tax advisor for your specific situation.
